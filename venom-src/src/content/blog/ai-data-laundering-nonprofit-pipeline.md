- AI data laundering is a structural mechanism, not a metaphor. A non-profit or academic entity scrapes the web under a research exception, releases the result under an open license, and commercial labs train on it while claiming reliance on that license. Each handoff sheds a layer of legal responsibility<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>
- The term was coined by Andy Baio in September 2022 to describe exactly this academic-to-commercial pipeline, where original creators are never asked for consent and no party in the chain holds direct liability for the scraping<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>
- In Kneschke v. The court held Kneschke's opt-out failed because it was not machine-readable, which means a machine-readable reservation can bind general commercial mining even where the research exception controlled here. A court has now validated the upstream half of the laundering structure<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>

VENOM analyzes the training-data supply chain through one distinction: preference signals (robots.txt, [AIPREF](/blog/understanding-aipref-ietf-standard/)) versus cost-imposing enforcement. Data laundering is worth analyzing because it defeats the signaling layer by construction. It is not a copyright footnote but a structural power mechanism: a way to move legal responsibility downstream while value moves upstream. This piece traces that mechanism, the case that validated its upstream half, and which defenses can actually reach a structure built to bypass signaling.

## The Mechanism

The conceptual model is a four-step pipeline; the operational realization usually runs through the five-layer deniability chain described in the next section. The pipeline runs in four steps. First, academic or non-profit researchers scrape the open web under a research exception. Second, they release the assembled dataset under an open-source license, usually on a public repository like Hugging Face. Third, commercial AI companies train on the dataset and avoid direct liability for the scraping, because they did not perform it. Fourth, the people whose work was scraped are never asked for consent and have no party to hold responsible<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>.

Andy Baio named this "AI data laundering" in September 2022. The framing is precise. A commercial lab that scraped a billion non-consented images itself would face the full copyright and reputational exposure of that act. The same lab that downloads a research dataset claims it relied in good faith on the dataset's stated license. The work is identical. The liability is not. The laundering is the transfer of legal responsibility away from the entity that benefits, achieved by inserting a research intermediary between the scrape and the model<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>.

This is distinct from the question of where training data comes from, and distinct from what scraping costs. It is about how legal responsibility is dissolved between the scraper and the model.

![The AI data laundering pipeline in four steps: academic researchers scrape under a research exception, the dataset is released under an open license on a public repository, commercial labs train on it without direct scraping liability, and original creators are never asked for consent.](/images/diagrams/laundering-pipeline.png)

## The Deniability Chain

The four-step version is the conceptual spine. In practice the full path from raw scraping to a "clean" training dataset is the operational realization of those same steps, and it usually passes through five layers, each adding deniability<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>.

The first layer is proxy services that rotate IP addresses and anonymize the origin of requests. The second is [scraping-as-a-service companies](/blog/scraping-economics-2026/) such as Bright Data and Oxylabs, which sell collection as a commodity. The third is academic researchers who collect under a research exception. The fourth is open repositories like Hugging Face and GitHub, where licensing is often unclear or absent. The fifth is the AI company, which trains on the result and claims reliance on the dataset's stated license.

Read the chain from the bottom up and the design becomes legible. The commercial lab points to the open license. The repository points to the uploader. The researcher points to the research exception. The scraping vendor points to its customer's stated purpose. The proxy points to nothing at all, because it has anonymized away the trail. No single layer holds the whole act. Responsibility is sheared off at every handoff, and what arrives at the model looks like a dataset rather than a scrape.

Not every laundered dataset uses all five layers. The point is structural. Each intermediary that sits between the creator and the model converts a question of liability into a question of provenance, and provenance is exactly what these repositories do not reliably track.

## LAION: The Canonical Case

LAION-5B is the clearest example. It contains roughly five billion image-text pairs and was created by LAION e.V., a German non-profit, with funding from commercial AI firms including Stability AI<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup><sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. That funding detail is the crux, and it is the cleanest power-asymmetry in the chain: the beneficiary funds the intermediary that absorbs the liability. Risk flows downstream, from the commercial lab onto the research organization that assembled the corpus and the creators whose work it ingested; value flows upstream, to the lab that trains on the result. LAION-5B went on to train Stable Diffusion, Google's Imagen, and Meta's Make-A-Video<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>.

The provenance is what the open license obscures. Baio's analysis of a 12-million-image sample traced content back to Pinterest (more than one million images), WordPress-hosted blogs (roughly 819,000), Flickr (roughly 121,000), and DeviantArt (roughly 67,000), none of whom agreed to AI-training use<sup><a href="#ref-1">1</a></sup>. The dataset does not host the images. It hosts URLs and captions, which is part of how it claims to sit inside a research exception. The images themselves still belong to the people who posted them.

Then comes the reason laundering is more than a copyright convenience. In December 2023 the Stanford Internet Observatory found 3,226 suspected links to child sexual abuse material in LAION-5B, of which 1,008 were externally validated<sup><a href="#ref-4">4</a></sup>. LAION took the dataset down for re-release. The finding is the sharpest demonstration of the incentive behind laundering. A commercial lab does not only want to avoid copyright liability for scraping. It wants distance from whatever ends up inside an unfiltered billion-item web corpus. A research intermediary that assembles the corpus absorbs that exposure first.

![LAION-5B provenance: five billion image-text pairs, with traced sources including over one million from Pinterest, 819,000 from WordPress blogs, 121,000 from Flickr, and 67,000 from DeviantArt, and 1,008 externally validated CSAM links found by Stanford.](/images/diagrams/laundering-laion-provenance.png)

## The Hamburg Ruling

A court has now validated the upstream half of this structure. In Kneschke v. LAION, the Hanseatic Higher Regional Court Hamburg (OLG Hamburg, case 5 U 104/24, decided December 10, 2025) affirmed the first-instance judgment of the Hamburg Regional Court (LG Hamburg, 310 O 227/23, September 2024) and held that LAION's creation of the dataset did not infringe copyright<sup><a href="#ref-3">3</a></sup>.

The decision turns on which text-and-data-mining exception applies. LAION's collection fell under the scientific-research exception, Section 60d of the German Copyright Act, transposing Article 3 of the EU Copyright in the Digital Single Market Directive, which permits research organizations to mine lawfully accessible works and which a rightsholder opt-out does not override. The general commercial TDM exception, Section 44b transposing Article 4, is different: it applies only where the rightsholder has not reserved use, and that reservation does take effect. The court held Kneschke's reservation failed under that test because it was not machine-readable. A human-readable disclaimer in general terms of use was not enough.

The ruling formally protects the exact node the laundering structure depends on. The research intermediary is shielded by the scientific-research exception even when commercial labs are the downstream beneficiaries, and that exception is the one an opt-out cannot reach. The court did not bless the commercial training that follows. It blessed the upstream collection, which is the half that the commercial labs cannot do for themselves.

![The Hamburg ruling split: the non-commercial research organization is protected upstream under the EU text-and-data-mining exception, while commercial labs reap the benefit downstream, with the ruling validating only the upstream collection.](/images/diagrams/laundering-hamburg-split.png)

## Text-Side Laundering: The Pile

Laundering is not unique to images. [The Pile](/blog/training-data-ecosystem-2026/), released by EleutherAI on December 31, 2020, is an 825 GiB text corpus and the text-side laundering vehicle<sup><a href="#ref-5">5</a></sup>. Two of its components show the pattern.

The first is Books3, a subset of 196,640 books scraped from the Bibliotik shadow library. Books3 was removed after a takedown notice from the Danish Rights Alliance, an EU rights-holder enforcement that the host complied with voluntarily in mid-2023<sup><a href="#ref-6">6</a></sup>. By then it had already been ingested into multiple models. This is the takedown-too-late problem. Laundered content persists in trained weights and in archived dataset copies long after the source is pulled. Removing the file does not remove the training.

The second is a "YouTube Subtitles" dataset of 173,536 transcribed videos, packaged into The Pile and reportedly used by Apple, Anthropic, Nvidia, and Salesforce. The transcription violated YouTube's Terms of Service, which bar access by automated means. No downstream company accepts direct responsibility, because none of them performed the scraping<sup><a href="#ref-7">7</a></sup>. The same deniability structure, applied to text.

## Why It Works, and Why It Is Contested

Two conditions keep the mechanism running. The first is the research exception, which gives the upstream collector a legal basis the commercial lab lacks. The second is weak provenance. License clarity on open dataset repositories is inconsistent. Many Hugging Face datasets lack a clear commercial-use license, and scraped content can enter without any provenance tracking at all<sup><a href="#ref-2">2</a></sup>. That ambiguity is what lets a downstream lab claim good-faith reliance on a dataset's stated license. If provenance were tracked end to end, good-faith reliance would be far harder to assert.

The contrast that sharpens the point is Common Crawl. Its crawler, CCBot, respects robots.txt and applies a generous request delay. It is the transparent, robots-compliant baseline of the training-data ecosystem. The laundering pipeline exists precisely because Common Crawl will not knowingly host shadow-library books, Terms-of-Service-violating YouTube transcripts, or non-consented image scrapes. A separate non-profit and academic channel supplies what the compliant crawler deliberately omits<sup><a href="#ref-9">9</a></sup>. Laundering routes around the compliant crawler, not through it.

The structure is contested. Hamburg protects the upstream non-profit under EU text-and-data-mining law, a posture tracked alongside the broader [EU AI Act GPAI obligations](/blog/eu-ai-act-gpai-2026/). In the United States, where the live [training-data litigation](/blog/litigation-tracker-2026/) is still resolving, the Copyright Office has concluded that using copyrighted works to train generative models implicates copyright and that training is not inherently transformative<sup><a href="#ref-8">8</a></sup>. Those two signals point in opposite directions. The upstream collection may be lawful in one jurisdiction while the downstream commercial use is unsettled in another. The laundering structure is not yet endorsed end to end by any single legal system.

## What This Means for Defense

In VENOM's enforcement-vs-signaling terms, the defensive question is which signal can reach which actor. The Hamburg ruling draws the line for us, and it is not the line the simple "robots.txt cannot reach laundering" story suggests.

Against general commercial mining, machine-readable signals have real teeth. The OLG Hamburg held that a machine-readable opt-out does bind Section 44b collection. Kneschke lost only because his reservation was human-readable. That is the case for [AIPREF](/blog/understanding-aipref-ietf-standard/)-style consent signals: where the collector relies on the commercial TDM exception, a machine-readable preference that travels with the content can defeat the legal basis directly. This is signaling that does enforcement work, because a statute attaches consequences to it.

The harder, genuinely open node is the scientific-research exception. Section 60d, the basis LAION won on, is read more broadly and an opt-out does not override it. A research crawler claiming that exception, and a downstream lab that never touches the publisher's server, both sit outside the reach of a robots.txt directive aimed at GPTBot or ClaudeBot, and a research-side opt-out may not bind them either. That is the specific layer signaling struggles to reach, not laundering wholesale.

Three remedies are sometimes proposed, and each carries a cost that the proposal usually omits. Consent signaling that travels with the content, through standards like the IETF AI preferences work, is the cheapest to publishers but, as above, bites the commercial exception more than the research one. Provenance tracking through the dataset chain would close the good-faith-reliance gap that license ambiguity opens, but it shifts the compliance burden onto repositories and uploaders and is unproven at billion-item scale; it is not clear it is even feasible at five-billion-item size. Value-degradation defenses operate on the content directly and survive the handoffs that strip away liability, but they impose a quality cost on the publisher's own legitimate users, who consume the same degraded content the scraper does.

None of these is a finished answer. The laundering structure is partly validated upstream, contested downstream, and routed around the one widely deployed signal that publishers control. VENOM's role here is to measure which enforcement mechanism actually reaches a structure built to bypass signaling, and to keep the Section 60d and Section 44b cases separate rather than collapsing them. The questions VENOM is measuring are whether provenance can be made to travel with content at scale, whether courts will treat downstream commercial reliance on a laundered dataset as a defense or as willful blindness, and whether a consent signal can bind the research intermediary the robots.txt layer cannot reach. Those are the questions worth measuring, not the ones worth assuming.
